Unveiling Patterns of Justified Violence Against Women: A Data-Driven Perspective

Abstarct

Violence against women and girls (VAWG) remains one of the most pervasive global human rights violations, often reinforced by cultural norms and justified attitudes. This poster presents a data-driven exploration of such attitudes using a cross-national dataset derived from Demographic and Health Surveys (DHS). The dataset includes responses from both men and women across African, Asian, and South American countries regarding their perceived justifications for acts of violence, segmented by demographic variables such as education, age, marital status, and employment. Using clustering techniques such as K-Means and Principal Component Analysis (PCA), we uncover latent patterns in country-level attitudes toward various forms of gender-based violence. Our analysis identifies distinct clusters of countries with similar justification profiles and highlights high-risk groups, particularly women with lower education levels and those in rural areas. The findings contribute to Sustainable Development Goal 5 and emphasize the potential of machine learning to illuminate bias, inform advocacy, and guide policy intervention
